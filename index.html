Training on Cyber Crime and Investigation

Organized by: Dhenkanal Police

Module 1: Network Fundamentals

1. OSI Model (The "How Data Travels" Map)

  • Full Form: Open Systems Interconnection Model.
  • What it is: A 7-layer framework that describes how a message moves from a human tapping a screen to a signal traveling through a wire.
  • Why it’s needed: It helps police identify where a crime occurred—was it a physical break-in (Layer 1) or a hacked WhatsApp account (Layer 7)?
  • General Location: It is a conceptual "rulebook" used by every computer in the world.
  • Investigation Location: When you seize a device, you look at Layer 7 logs (Application logs like WhatsApp, Instagram).
  • How to read it: Layer 1 is the "Ground" (Cables) and Layer 7 is the "Top" (The App).
  • Data Link (Layer 2): This is where the MAC Address lives; it connects the hardware to the local network.
  • Network (Layer 3): This is where the IP Address lives; it helps the data find the right "digital house."
  • Transport (Layer 4): This ensures the message arrives in the right order and isn't broken.
  • The Analogy: Think of it like a courier service. Layer 1 is the road, Layer 3 is the address on the box, and Layer 7 is the letter inside.

2. IP Addresses (The "Digital House Number")

  • Full Form: Internet Protocol Address.
  • What it is: A unique string of numbers assigned to every device on a network.
  • Why it’s needed: It is the first clue in any cybercrime. It tells you which internet connection was used to commit the act.
  • General Location: Found in your phone’s "Network Settings" or by searching "What is my IP" on Google.
  • Investigation Location: Found in the server logs of the victim (e.g., the IP address that logged into a stolen bank account).
  • IPv4 Appearance: Four sets of numbers separated by dots (e.g., 103.45.2.11).
  • IPv6 Appearance: Eight groups of letters and numbers (e.g., 2001:db8:0:0:0:ff00:42:8329).
  • Public IP: The address the whole world sees (assigned by the ISP).
  • Private IP: The address used inside a home/office (assigned by the local router).
  • Key Insight: A Public IP identifies the house/office, while a Private IP identifies the specific device inside that house.

3. Static vs. Dynamic IPs (The "Changing Address")

  • What it is: Static means the IP never changes. Dynamic means the IP changes every time the user restarts their phone or router.
  • Why it’s needed: To understand why an IP address you found yesterday might belong to a different person today.
  • The Indian Context: Most mobile users in India (JIO, Airtel) use Dynamic IPs to save resources.
  • Investigation Challenge: If you track a Dynamic IP without a timestamp, you might arrest the wrong person.
  • Static Use Case: Usually used by big companies, banks, or police headquarters so their servers are always easy to find.
  • Lease Time: In Dynamic IPs, a user "rents" an IP for a few hours. This is called a Lease.
  • General Location: This setting is managed by the ISP's main server.
  • Investigation Location: Found in the Radius Logs or DHCP Logs of the Telecom Company.
  • How to read it: Look for the "Allocation Time" and "Release Time" in the ISP report.
  • Critical Step: Always ask the ISP: "Who was using this IP at exactly 14:05:10 IST on Oct 12?"

4. MAC Address (The "Hardware Fingerprint")

  • Full Form: Media Access Control Address.
  • What it is: A permanent, physical ID number burned into the Wi-Fi or Ethernet chip of a device at the factory.
  • Why it’s needed: Even if a suspect changes their IP or SIM card, the MAC address of the laptop/phone remains the same.
  • General Location: On a sticker at the back of a laptop or under the "About Phone" menu.
  • Investigation Location: Found in the logs of the Wi-Fi router used by the suspect.
  • How it looks: Six pairs of characters (e.g., 00:0A:95:9D:68:16).
  • NIC (Network Interface Card): The physical part inside the phone that has the MAC address.
  • Manufacturer Clue: The first three pairs (e.g., 00:0A:95) tell you which company made the device (Apple, Samsung, etc.).
  • Spoofing: Tech-savvy criminals can "fake" a MAC address using software, though the physical one stays the same.
  • Evidence Value: Linking a MAC address found in a crime scene router to a laptop found in a suspect’s house is a "smoking gun."

5. DHCP Logs (The "Record Book")

  • Full Form: Dynamic Host Configuration Protocol Logs.
  • What it is: A digital diary kept by a router or ISP that records which IP was given to which device.
  • Why it’s needed: It is the bridge that connects an IP Address to a MAC Address.
  • General Location: Inside the memory of a Wi-Fi router or the ISP's database.
  • Investigation Location: You seize the router from a suspect’s house and download these logs.
  • How to read it: It looks like a spreadsheet with columns for "Time," "MAC Address," and "Assigned IP."
  • Reservation: Sometimes a router is set to always give the same IP to the "Boss’s Laptop." This is a "DHCP Reservation."
  • Scope: This is the range of IPs a router is allowed to give out (e.g., 192.168.1.2 to 192.168.1.100).
  • Lease Duration: Tells you how long the suspect was connected to that specific Wi-Fi.
  • Legal Use: These logs prove that Device A was present on Network B at Time C.

6. Subnetting & Gateway (The "Local Map")

  • Subnetting: Dividing a large network into smaller "neighborhoods" (subnets).
  • Gateway: The "Main Gate" or router that connects a local network to the internet.
  • Why it’s needed: To narrow down where a suspect is. Are they in the "Accounting Subnet" or the "Guest Wi-Fi Subnet"?
  • General Location: Found in the "Status" or "IP Config" settings of any computer.
  • Investigation Location: Helps you find the physical router (Gateway) that needs to be seized.
  • How it looks: A Subnet Mask usually looks like 255.255.255.0.
  • The Gateway IP: Usually ends in .1 (e.g., 192.168.1.1). This is the router's internal address.
  • Public Gateway: For an ISP, this is the "Tower" or "Exchange" the user is connected to.
  • IP Conflict: If two devices have the same IP on one subnet, the internet stops working—a clue that a new device just joined.
  • Officer’s Goal: Find the Gateway IP to find the physical hardware controlling the traffic.

7. IMEI vs. IMSI (The "Phone vs. SIM")

  • IMEI Full Form: International Mobile Equipment Identity.
  • IMSI Full Form: International Mobile Subscriber Identity.
  • IMEI (The Phone): A 15-digit number unique to the handset. It doesn't change if you change the SIM.
  • IMSI (The SIM): A 15-digit number unique to the SIM card/Subscriber. It travels with the SIM to new phones.
  • Why it’s needed: To track a person even if they switch phones (track IMSI) or switch SIMs (track IMEI).
  • General Location: IMEI: Dial *#06# or check the box. IMSI: Stored inside the SIM card.
  • Investigation Location: Found in CDRs (Call Detail Records) provided by companies like JIO/Airtel.
  • How to read: IMEI usually starts with 35... or 86.... IMSI in India usually starts with 404 or 405.
  • Blacklisting: If a phone is stolen, the police "Blacklist" the IMEI so no SIM card will work in it.
  • The Link: Every time a call is made, the tower records BOTH. This links a specific person to a specific handset.

8. Cell Tower Triangulation (The "Search Area")

  • What it is: Using the signal strength from 3 or more towers to calculate a suspect's location.
  • Why it’s needed: To find a suspect’s live location even if they have turned off GPS/Location services.
  • How it works: The phone is always "talking" to the nearest towers to stay connected.
  • Accuracy: In cities (many towers), it is very accurate (within 50-100 meters). In villages, it may only be within 2-3 kilometers.
  • General Location: Only accessible by the Telecom Service Provider (TSP).
  • Investigation Location: You receive a Cell Site Map from the TSP.
  • Azimuth/Sector: Towers have three sides (sectors). The log tells you which "side" of the tower the suspect was on.
  • Timing Advance (TA): A value that tells you exactly how many meters the suspect was from the tower.
  • Handover: When a suspect is moving in a car, they "handover" from one tower to another. This shows their direction of travel.
  • Evidence: Used to prove a suspect was "present at the crime scene" at the time of the incident.

9. Wi-Fi SSID & BSSID (The "Wireless Clues")

  • SSID Full Form: Service Set Identifier (The name you see, like "Home_WiFi").
  • BSSID Full Form: Basic Service Set Identifier (The MAC address of that specific router).
  • Why it’s needed: A suspect can name their Wi-Fi "Police_Station" (SSID) to prank people, but the BSSID will always be a unique serial number.
  • General Location: Visible on any phone when searching for Wi-Fi.
  • Investigation Location: Check the "Saved Networks" on a suspect's seized phone. It shows everywhere they have been.
  • BSSID Mapping: Websites like Wigle.net keep a global map of BSSIDs. You can search a BSSID and see exactly where that router is located.
  • Public Hotspots: If a crime was committed using "Railway_Free_WiFi," the BSSID tells you exactly which platform the suspect was standing on.
  • Hidden SSID: Some criminals hide their Wi-Fi name. You need special tools (like Wireshark) to see these.
  • RSSI: This is the signal strength. A high RSSI means the suspect was standing very close to the router.
  • Evidence Value: Proves the suspect's device was connected to a specific router at a specific location.

10. VPN / Proxy Mechanics (The "Mask")

  • Full Form: Virtual Private Network.
  • What it is: An app that creates an encrypted "tunnel." It hides your real Indian IP and replaces it with a foreign IP (e.g., USA).
  • Why it’s needed: To understand why a "Threat Mail" appears to come from New York when the suspect is in Mumbai.
  • Proxy vs. VPN: A Proxy only hides your IP for one app (like a browser). A VPN hides everything on the phone.
  • The "Middle-Man": The data goes Suspect -> VPN Server -> Internet. The internet only sees the VPN Server's IP.
  • Logs: Most VPNs claim "No-Logs" policy, making it very hard for police to get the real IP.
  • General Location: Look for apps like NordVPN, ExpressVPN, or "Turbo Proxy" on the suspect’s phone.
  • Investigation Clue: If you see an IP address belonging to "Amazon Data Center" or "Google Cloud" instead of a home ISP, it is likely a VPN/Proxy.
  • Encryption: VPNs scramble the data. Even if you intercept the Wi-Fi signal, you can’t read the messages.
  • Officer’s Strategy: If a VPN is used, look for "Physical Evidence" (like a logged-in session on a laptop) rather than trying to track the IP through the foreign VPN company.
Module 2: Web & Domain Investigation

1. DNS (Domain Name System)

  • What it is: The "Phonebook of the Internet." It translates human names (google.com) into computer numbers (142.250.190.46).
  • Why it’s needed: If you have a scam website name, DNS records tell you which IP address it is currently pointing to.
  • A-Record: The most common record; it maps the domain name directly to an IPv4 address.
  • CNAME: An alias record. It shows if "https://www.google.com/search?q=scam-site.com" is actually just a mask for another hidden website.
  • MX-Record: Shows the "Mail Exchange" server. This tells you which service the criminal is using to send emails.
  • NS-Record: "Name Server" records show which company is managing the website's directions (e.g., Cloudflare or GoDaddy).
  • DNS Cache: Computers "remember" DNS for a while. Even if a criminal deletes a site, the "cache" might still show the old IP.
  • General Location: Publicly available through "DNS Lookup" tools online.
  • Investigation Use: If a site is taken down, you look at "Historical DNS" to see where it used to be hosted.
  • How to read it: Domain: scam.com -> Points to IP: 1.2.3.4.

2. WHOIS Lookup

  • What it is: A public database query that asks, "Who owns this domain?"
  • Why it’s needed: To find the name, email, and phone number of the person who bought the website.
  • Privacy Protection: Many criminals use "WhoisGuard" or "Privacy Protect" to hide their real name in public results.
  • Data Included: Registration date, Expiry date, and the "Registrar" (the shop where they bought it).
  • Technical Contact: Often shows the email of the person who set up the site—a major lead for police.
  • Creation Date: If a banking site was created only 2 days ago, it is almost certainly a fake/phishing site.
  • General Location: Websites like whois.com or centralops.net.
  • Investigation Location: If details are hidden, you must send a legal notice to the Registrar to get the "Underlying Subscriber Info."
  • Updated Date: Shows when the criminal last changed their settings or renewed the site.
  • How to read it: Look for the "Registrant Name" and "Registrant Email" fields.

3. Domain Registrars

  • What it is: The "Shop" or "Company" where a person buys a domain name (e.g., GoDaddy, Namecheap, BigRock).
  • Why it’s needed: They are the legal entity you send a notice to when you want to suspend/block a scam website.
  • The Registry: Above the registrar is the Registry (like Verisign for .com). Registrars are the middle-men.
  • Abuse Contact: Every registrar has an "abuse@" email address specifically for police to report scams.
  • KYC: Registrars keep credit card or PayPal details of the buyer. This is how you find the money trail.
  • Country of Origin: If a site is registered with a Russian registrar, it is harder for Indian police to get data than with an Indian registrar.
  • Resellers: Sometimes a small shop sells a domain through a big company. You have to trace the chain.
  • General Location: Found by looking at the "Registrar" field in a WHOIS lookup.
  • Investigation Use: You ask them for the "Payment Method" used to buy the domain.
  • Action: You can request a "Domain Freeze" so the criminal cannot move the site to another company.

4. Web Hosting (Shared vs. VPS)

  • What it is: The "Digital Land" where the website's files (images, code, stolen data) are stored.
  • Shared Hosting: Like an apartment building; many websites live on one server. Cheap and common for small scams.
  • VPS (Virtual Private Server): Like a private townhouse; more control and harder to investigate because the criminal has "Admin" rights.
  • Why it’s needed: The "Hosting Provider" holds the actual evidence (the stolen usernames/passwords).
  • General Location: Identified by the IP address of the website.
  • Investigation Use: You send a notice to the host to "Preserve Logs" and "Mirror the Image" (copy the files) for evidence.
  • Server Logs: The host records every IP that visited the site—this helps you find the victim list.
  • Physical Location: A site might be .in (India) but hosted on a server in the USA.
  • Datacenters: Companies like AWS, Google Cloud, or DigitalOcean are common hosts.
  • How to read it: Use a "Reverse IP Lookup" to see all other sites sharing that same server.

5. HTTP/HTTPS Headers

  • What it is: Hidden "metadata" sent between a browser and a server during every visit.
  • User-Agent: A header that reveals the suspect's Browser (Chrome/Safari) and OS (Windows 11/Android 14).
  • Why it’s needed: It helps narrow down the device. If the log shows "iPhone 15," but the suspect only has a Nokia, you look for a hidden device.
  • Referer: (Spelled with one 'r' in tech) Shows which website the user came from before hitting the current page.
  • Server Header: Tells you what software the criminal's server is running (e.g., Apache or Nginx).
  • Set-Cookie: Information the server puts on the suspect's phone to "remember" them.
  • Investigation Use: If a criminal visits a "honey pot" (a trap site set by police), these headers identify their setup.
  • X-Forwarded-For: A special header that can sometimes reveal a suspect's real IP even if they are using a proxy.
  • General Location: Found using "Inspect Element" in a browser or through network interception.
  • How to read it: User-Agent: Mozilla/5.0 (Windows NT 10.0; Win64; x64)...

6. SSL/TLS Certificates

  • What it is: The "Green Padlock" (HTTPS) that encrypts data.
  • Why it’s needed: To check if a site is "Validated." Scammers often use free certificates (like Let's Encrypt).
  • Full Form: Secure Sockets Layer / Transport Layer Security.
  • Issuer: The company that gave the certificate. If "State Bank" site is issued by "FreeSSL-Now," it is a fake.
  • Validity Period: Certificates usually last 90 days or 1 year. Scams have very recent "Start Dates."
  • Subject Alternative Name (SAN): Shows other websites owned by the same certificate holder—helping you find a "network" of scam sites.
  • General Location: Click the padlock icon in the browser address bar.
  • Investigation Use: If you find a certificate serial number, you can ask the issuer for the account details of the person who requested it.
  • Fingerprint: A unique code (SHA-256) for that certificate. You can search this code to find other identical fake sites.
  • Misconception: "HTTPS" does not mean a site is safe; it just means the connection is private. Even a thief can have a padlock on their door.

7. URL Shorteners

  • What it is: Services that turn long links into tiny ones (e.g., bit.ly/xxxx).
  • Why it’s needed: Criminals use them to hide the "https://www.google.com/search?q=scam-site.com" name in SMS/WhatsApp messages.
  • De-masking: You can see the real destination by adding a + sign at the end of many shortened links (e.g., bit.ly/xxxx+).
  • Analytics: Most shorteners keep public stats on how many people clicked and from which country.
  • Investigation Use: You contact the service (like Bitly or TinyURL) to get the IP address of the person who created the link.
  • Redirection: The link sends the user to the destination, but the "Shortener" records the click first.
  • Custom Branded Links: Some companies use their own (e.g., amzn.to for Amazon). If a link looks "weird," it is likely a scam.
  • General Location: Found in the body of phishing SMS or emails.
  • Phishing Prevention: Many shorteners will block a link if it is reported as a scam.
  • How to read it: It's just a 5-7 character code after a domain name.

8. ICANN Protocols

  • Full Form: Internet Corporation for Assigned Names and Numbers.
  • What it is: The global "Government" of the internet that sets rules for domains.
  • Why it’s needed: When a criminal uses a .com or .net domain, they must follow ICANN's "Registrant Agreement."
  • UDRP: (Uniform Domain-Name Dispute-Resolution Policy) A process to take back a domain if someone is impersonating a brand (like sbi-bank-login.com).
  • Whois Accuracy: ICANN rules state that if a user provides "False WHOIS data," the registrar must cancel the domain.
  • Police Role: You can use ICANN's "Accuracy Complaint" to get a scam site taken down quickly if the owner's info is fake.
  • Global Jurisdiction: ICANN is based in the USA, but its rules apply to all registrars worldwide.
  • General Location: Rules are found on icann.org.
  • GNSO: The group within ICANN that makes rules for generic domains (like .com).
  • Key Insight: ICANN doesn't remove "Content" (like a bad video), they only handle "Domains" (the name).

9. Robots.txt & Sitemap

  • Robots.txt: A file that tells Google which parts of a website not to show in search results.
  • Sitemap: A "List of Contents" for the website meant for search engines.
  • Why it’s needed: Criminals often hide their "Admin Panel" or "Stolen Data Folder" in the robots.txt so the public can't find it via Google.
  • Investigation Use: By reading these files, you can find hidden pages like /admin/login or /uploads/stolen_cards/.
  • General Location: Found by adding it to the end of a URL (e.g., scam-site.com/robots.txt).
  • How to read it: Look for the word Disallow:. Whatever follows it is a hidden folder.
  • Sitemap XML: Usually found at scam-site.com/sitemap.xml. It lists every single page on the site.
  • Manual Discovery: Sometimes criminals forget to protect these folders, allowing police to download all evidence without a password.
  • Clues: A sitemap might show the date and time each page was created, helping build a timeline of the crime.
  • Observation: If a site has no robots.txt or sitemap, it might be a very crude, "hit-and-run" scam.

10. CDN (Content Delivery Networks)

  • What it is: A service like Cloudflare that acts as a "Shield" for a website.
  • Why it’s needed: It speeds up the site but also hides the true IP address of the server.
  • The "Mask": When you ping a Cloudflare-protected site, you see Cloudflare's IP, not the criminal's IP.
  • Direct-IP Attack: Investigation goal is to find the "Origin IP" (the real server hiding behind the shield).
  • Cloudflare Resolver: Tools that look for "leaks" where the server accidentally revealed its real IP.
  • Legal Notice: You must send a notice to Cloudflare asking for the "Origin IP" and "Payment Details."
  • WAF: (Web Application Firewall) CDNs provide this to block police "scanning" tools.
  • General Location: Identified if the Name Servers (NS) are ashley.ns.cloudflare.com or similar.
  • Investigation Challenge: Criminals love CDNs because they provide a "free" layer of anonymity.
  • How to read it: If the IP lookup says "Organization: Cloudflare Net," you know the real host is hidden
Module 3: Telecom Records & Field Evidence

1. CDR (Call Detail Record)

  • Full Form: Call Detail Record.
  • What it is: A digital log of every call and SMS sent or received by a specific mobile number.
  • Why it’s needed: To establish a relationship between suspects. It proves who talked to whom, for how long, and how often.
  • General Location: Stored in the TSP’s main billing and network servers.
  • Investigation Location: You obtain this by sending a formal request to the Nodal Officer of the telecom company.
  • Data Included: Caller number, Receiver number, Date, Time, Duration, and the Cell Tower ID used.
  • What’s Missing: CDRs do not contain the audio recording of the call or the text content of an SMS.
  • SMS Records: It shows the time an SMS was sent, but the "Duration" column will usually be "0" or "1".
  • Pattern Analysis: High frequency of calls at odd hours (2 AM) between two numbers suggests a strong conspiracy.
  • How to read it: It is usually an Excel sheet. Look for "A-Party" (the caller) and "B-Party" (the receiver).

2. IPDR (Internet Protocol Detail Record)

  • Full Form: Internet Protocol Detail Record.
  • What it is: A log of every time a mobile user connects to the internet.
  • Why it’s needed: This is the most critical log for cybercrime. It maps a "Public IP" (found in a crime) back to a specific mobile number.
  • The Link: If a hacker uses an IP at 10:00 AM, the IPDR tells you exactly which phone number was assigned that IP at that second.
  • General Location: Maintained by the ISP/TSP for at least 2 years as per Govt. norms.
  • Investigation Location: Requested from the TSP when you have an IP address but no phone number.
  • Data Included: Mobile Number, Private IP, Public IP, Start Time, End Time, and often the MAC Address.
  • Destination IP: Some IPDRs also show which website (Destination IP) the suspect visited (e.g., Facebook’s server IP).
  • Data Volume: It shows how many MBs or GBs of data were uploaded or downloaded.
  • How to read it: Look for the "Source IP" column and match it with the "Username/Mobile Number" column.

3. SDR (Subscriber Detail Record)

  • Full Form: Subscriber Detail Record.
  • What it is: The digital profile of the person who owns the SIM card.
  • Why it’s needed: To get the name, address, and identity proof of the person holding the mobile number.
  • General Location: The TSP’s customer database.
  • Investigation Location: Requested alongside the CDR to identify the suspect’s name.
  • Data Included: Name, Father’s Name, Permanent Address, Alternate Mobile Number, and Date of Activation.
  • KYC Link: It contains the unique ID number of the document used (e.g., Aadhaar Number).
  • Photo: The SDR usually includes the digital photo taken at the time of purchasing the SIM.
  • Verification: Always check the "Alternate Number"—criminals often use their real number as an alternate for a fake SIM.
  • How to read it: It looks like a profile page or a summary table of the user's personal details.

4. GPRS / Data Logs

  • Full Form: General Packet Radio Service Logs.
  • What it is: A record of when the "Mobile Data" toggle was switched ON or OFF on a phone.
  • Why it’s needed: To prove if a suspect was "Online" during the time a digital crime (like a WhatsApp threat) was sent.
  • General Location: Stored in the TSP’s data core network.
  • Investigation Location: Often included as part of the IPDR or as a separate "Data Usage Report."
  • Session Duration: It shows how long a single internet session lasted (e.g., 4 hours).
  • APN (Access Point Name): Shows the setting used to connect (e.g., 'www' or 'internet').
  • Volume: Shows if the user was just browsing (small data) or downloading heavy files/videos (large data).
  • Roaming: Indicates if the data was used while the suspect was in a different state/circle.
  • How to read it: Look for "Upload Bytes" and "Download Bytes" to see the level of activity.

5. Tower Dumps

  • What it is: A list of every mobile number that connected to a specific cell tower during a specific time window.
  • Why it’s needed: If a murder happens in a park at 9 PM, you pull the "Tower Dump" to see which phones were present in that park at that time.
  • General Location: Telecom Tower logs.
  • Investigation Location: You provide the Cell ID (from a victim’s CDR) and a time range (e.g., 8 PM to 10 PM) to the TSP.
  • Analysis: You compare dumps from different locations. If "Number X" appears at the victim’s house AND the disposal site, that’s your suspect.
  • Data Size: These files are huge, often containing thousands of numbers.
  • Filter: You can filter by "Entry" and "Exit" to see who arrived just before the crime and left immediately after.
  • Identity: Once you find a suspicious number in the dump, you then request the SDR for that number.
  • Limitations: It won't show people whose phones were switched off or in Airplane Mode.
  • How to read it: Usually a massive Excel CSV file with columns for "Mobile Number" and "Time of Connection."

6. CAF (Customer Application Form)

  • Full Form: Customer Application Form.
  • What it is: The physical or digital form filled out by the customer at the mobile shop.
  • Why it’s needed: It contains the Physical Signature of the suspect and the address of the shop where the SIM was bought.
  • General Location: Scanned copies are kept in the TSP’s digital archives; physical copies are in their warehouses.
  • Investigation Location: Requested when you suspect the SIM was bought using a fake ID (you can check the handwriting/signature).
  • Point of Sale (PoS): The form shows the "Retailer Code." You can visit that shop to check CCTV or interview the shopkeeper.
  • Date of Sale: Helps verify if the SIM was pre-activated (illegal) or bought specifically for the crime.
  • Proof of Address (PoA): Shows the actual photocopy of the ID card (Aadhaar/Voter ID) submitted.
  • Digital CAF (e-CAF): Modern forms use biometric (thumbprint) authentication, which is harder to forge.
  • How to read it: It’s a scanned image of the document the suspect touched and signed.

7. MNP History

  • Full Form: Mobile Number Portability History.
  • What it is: A record of whether a phone number was moved from one provider to another (e.g., Airtel to Jio).
  • Why it’s needed: If you send a request to Airtel but the user ported to Jio, Airtel will have no recent data.
  • General Location: A central database managed by MNP Interconnection companies.
  • Investigation Location: You can check this instantly using online "HLR Lookup" tools or by asking any TSP.
  • Porting Date: Tells you when the service changed.
  • UPC (Unique Porting Code): The code generated to switch networks—this can sometimes be used to track the request source.
  • Network Circle: Confirms if the number belongs to "Delhi," "Maharashtra," etc., even after porting.
  • Investigative Tip: If a suspect ports their number frequently, they may be trying to confuse law enforcement.
  • How to read it: It usually says "Original Network: Vi | Current Network: Jio | Ported on: 12-Jan-2024."

8. SMS Headers (The Phishing Clue)

  • What it is: The 6-character alphabetic code that appears instead of a number (e.g., AD-HDFCBK).
  • Why it’s needed: To identify the "Bulk SMS Gateway" used by scammers to send fake bank alerts or OTP scams.
  • Full Form: Not a specific full form, but often referred to as a "Sender ID."
  • General Location: Regulated by TRAI in India under the DLT (Distributed Ledger Technology) system.
  • Investigation Location: You look at the victim’s phone. The header (e.g., BZ-VICTIM) tells you which company sent it.
  • Structure: The first letter (B) is the TSP (BSNL), the second (Z) is the city (Mumbai). The rest is the company name.
  • Tracing: You contact the TSP to find which "Telemarketer" is registered to use that specific header.
  • DLT Platform: Every header must be registered. You can find the owner of a header through the TRAI portal.
  • Content Templates: Scammers often hijack "approved templates" to make their fake SMS look real.
  • How to read it: XY-ABCDEF. X=Service Provider, Y=Service Area, ABCDEF=Sender Name.

9. VoIP Tracing (WhatsApp/Skype Calls)

  • Full Form: Voice over Internet Protocol.
  • What it is: Calls made using the internet instead of traditional cellular lines.
  • Why it’s needed: Criminals use WhatsApp or Telegram calls because they are encrypted and don't show up in a standard CDR.
  • The Challenge: A standard CDR will only show "Data Usage," not the person the suspect talked to on WhatsApp.
  • Investigation Location: You must request data from the App Provider (Meta for WhatsApp) using an LER (Law Enforcement Request).
  • IP Evidence: VoIP calls leave "IP logs." You can see the IP address the suspect was connected to during the call.
  • Virtual Numbers: Criminals use apps to get "USA Numbers" (+1) to make VoIP calls while sitting in India.
  • Gateway Tracing: If a VoIP call is "terminated" on a landline, it goes through a gateway that can be traced.
  • Wi-Fi Link: If the VoIP call was made over Wi-Fi, the router’s logs will show the connection to the VoIP server.
  • How to read it: In a CDR, VoIP looks like a long "GPRS Session" with no B-Party number.

10. IMSI Catchers (The "Fake Tower")

  • What it is: A device (often called a "Stingray") that acts like a fake cell tower to trick nearby phones into connecting to it.
  • Why it’s needed (Knowledge): To understand how advanced criminals or foreign spies might intercept calls or locate a person without TSP help.
  • How it works: It sends a stronger signal than the real tower. Phones automatically connect to the strongest signal.
  • Data Interception: Once connected, the catcher can see the IMSI and IMEI of every phone in the area.
  • Investigation Use: Police use these (with high-level permission) to find a specific suspect in a crowded building.
  • The Red Flag: If many people in one area complain of "dropped calls" or their "4G falling to 2G," an IMSI catcher might be nearby.
  • Detection: Special "Network Monitor" apps can sometimes detect if a tower is fake (it will have no "Neighboring Cell" info).
  • Legal Status: Use of these is highly restricted and usually requires Home Ministry clearance.
  • Encrypted Apps: Even an IMSI catcher cannot easily read messages from WhatsApp or Signal because they are encrypted end-to-end.
  • How it looks: It is a physical hardware box with antennas, often hidden in a vehicle or a briefcase.
Module 4: Social Media & OSINT

1. Facebook/Instagram LEO Portals

  • Full Form: Law Enforcement Online Portal.
  • What it is: A secure, "cops-only" website where police submit legal requests directly to Meta (the company that owns FB/Instagram).
  • Why it’s needed: To get private data like IP addresses, login history, and linked phone numbers that aren't visible on a public profile.
  • General Location: facebook.com/records. You must register using your official gov.in or nic.in email ID.
  • Emergency Requests: The portal has a "Life Safety" option for kidnappings or suicide threats to get data in minutes instead of weeks.
  • Data Provided: Includes "Basic Subscriber Info" (Name, Email, Phone, Registration Date).
  • Preservation: You can click "Preserve" to tell Meta to save a suspect's data for 90 days while you get a court order.
  • Investigation Use: Proving that the person who posted a threat is the same person who owns the mobile number.
  • How it looks: A dashboard where you upload your signed Section 91 (or BNSS 94) notice as a PDF.
  • The Key: Meta will only talk to you through this portal; they rarely respond to random emails.

2. X (Twitter) Advanced Search

  • What it is: A powerful search tool that filters tweets by date, location, and specific words.
  • Why it’s needed: To find a suspect's deleted "history" or old conversations they forgot they had years ago.
  • The Logic: You can search for: (from:SuspectUser) until:2020-01-01 to see their old mindset.
  • Mentions: You can see who the suspect talked to most by searching to:SuspectUser.
  • Location: Accessible via the "three dots" next to the search bar on X.com or twitter.com/search-advanced.
  • Incriminating Tweets: Criminals often delete evidence, but "replies" from other people usually stay visible.
  • Media Search: You can filter for only "Videos" or "Photos" posted by a suspect in a specific city.
  • Investigation Use: Building a "Timeline" of a suspect's radicalization or criminal planning.
  • Archiving: Once you find a tweet, take a screenshot immediately or use a tool to save the link.
  • How to read it: It looks like a standard search but gives you much more specific results.

3. Telegram Bot Analysis

  • What it is: Automated programs inside Telegram that can "sell" data, leak phone numbers, or spread malware.
  • Why it’s needed: Many Indian "Data Leaks" (Aadhaar/Voter ID) are sold via these bots for a few rupees.
  • BotFather: The main system that creates these bots. If you catch a suspect's phone, look for "BotFather" in their chats.
  • API Key: The "Secret Code" that controls the bot. If you find this key, you can control the bot.
  • Payment Logs: Many bots use "Crypto" or "UPI" for payments. This is a major lead.
  • Command History: Bots respond to commands like /start or /search. Check what commands the suspect sent.
  • Scraping: Some bots are used to "scrape" (steal) contact lists from unsuspecting users.
  • Investigation Location: Found by searching the "Global Search" in Telegram for keywords like "Data Leak" or "OTP Bypass."
  • Anonymous: Telegram is very hard to get data from, so the "Bot" itself is often your only evidence.
  • How to read it: It looks like a chat window with a "Start" button at the bottom.

4. WhatsApp Metadata & "First Originator"

  • What it is: Metadata is "data about data"—who sent it, when, and to which group (not the content).
  • First Originator: Under Indian IT Rules, WhatsApp must identify the person who first started a viral fake news message.
  • Why it’s needed: To find the "Mastermind" of a riot or a scam, not just the people who forwarded it.
  • Group Admins: The person who creates the group or has the power to add/remove members.
  • The Challenge: WhatsApp is "End-to-End Encrypted," meaning you can't read the messages unless you have the physical phone.
  • Web Sessions: Check if "WhatsApp Web" is active; this shows the suspect is using a PC to manage the scam.
  • Investigation Use: In a seized phone, go to "Group Info" to see the "Created By" number and date.
  • IP Logs: WhatsApp records the IP address used for every login.
  • Status Updates: Don't forget to check "Status" history—suspects often post photos of their location or wealth there.
  • Evidence: A screenshot is not enough; you need the digital "Hash Value" (a unique fingerprint) of the message.

5. Snapchat Geofilters & Snap Map

  • What it is: A feature that shows a user's real-time location on a map or attaches a "city-specific" filter to a photo.
  • Why it’s needed: Criminals love to "flex" (show off) their location. The Snap Map can show exactly which building they are in.
  • Geofilter: If a suspect posts a photo with a "Connaught Place" filter, you know exactly where they were.
  • "Our Story": Public photos posted to a location. If a crime happened at a protest, check "Our Story" for that area.
  • Ghost Mode: If a suspect is smart, they turn this on to stay invisible on the map.
  • Investigation Use: Tracking a runaway or a suspect by seeing where their "Bitmoji" icon is on the map.
  • Retention: Snapchat deletes photos after viewing, but they keep metadata (logs) for a short time.
  • Account Link: Snapchat is often linked to a "Bitmoji," which can reveal the suspect's physical appearance.
  • How to read it: Open the Snap Map and look for "Heat Map" (blue/red spots) where many people are posting.
  • Legal: Like Meta, Snapchat has a portal for police to request location history.

6. LinkedIn Scraping

  • What it is: Extracting professional data (Job history, Education, Skills) from a LinkedIn profile.
  • Why it’s needed: High-level fraudsters (Investment Scammers) often create fake "CEO" profiles to look trustworthy.
  • Connections: Check who is "Endorsing" the suspect. These could be their partners in crime.
  • Company Page: See if the company they claim to work for actually exists or has real employees.
  • Profile History: LinkedIn tells you if a person changed their name recently.
  • Email Discovery: Sometimes users put their email or phone number in the "About" or "Contact Info" section.
  • Investigation Use: Identifying the real identity of a "Cyber Expert" or "Financial Advisor" who is actually a scammer.
  • Premium View: Be careful—LinkedIn tells the user "Officer Raj saw your profile" unless you use a "Sock Puppet" (hidden) account.
  • Search Filters: You can search for "Ex-Employees" of a scam company to find witnesses.
  • How to read it: Treat it like a digital Resume.

7. Reverse Image Search

  • What it is: Putting a photo into a search engine to find where else that same photo appears on the internet.
  • Tools: Google Images, Yandex (great for faces), and PimEyes (scary-accurate facial recognition).
  • Why it’s needed: To see if a suspect's profile picture is "Stolen" from a model or a real person's Instagram.
  • The "Fake" Clue: If you search a "Girl's Profile Pic" and find it belongs to a Russian actress, the profile is a honey-trap.
  • Original Source: It helps find the first time a photo was posted, which might lead to the suspect's real name.
  • Location Finding: If a suspect posts a photo of a balcony, search the photo to see if it matches a specific hotel or building.
  • Investigation Use: Identifying unidentified victims or unknown suspects from CCTV stills.
  • General Location: images.google.com.
  • EXIF Data: Some search engines can still read the hidden "Camera Data" in a photo (see point 11).
  • How to do it: Right-click an image and select "Search Image with Google."

8. Social Media Header Analysis

  • What it is: Checking the "Security Alerts" or "Login History" emails sent by social media apps.
  • Why it’s needed: When a suspect logs into Instagram, Meta sends an email: "New Login from IP 1.2.3.4."
  • Investigation Use: If you seize the suspect's email, you can find their IP address history without even talking to Instagram.
  • Device Info: The header shows if they logged in from an "iPhone" or "Windows PC."
  • Timestamp: It gives you the exact second of the login.
  • Location Estimate: The email often says "Near Mumbai, India."
  • General Location: Found in the "Security" or "Login Activity" tab of the app settings.
  • Phishing Clue: If a victim says they were hacked, check these headers to see the hacker's IP.
  • SMTP Logs: (See point 15) The email itself contains "Route" info of how it reached the inbox.
  • How to read it: Look for the line that says Source IP or Logged in from.

9. Archiving Evidence (Wayback Machine)

  • What it is: A "Time Machine" for the internet that saves copies of websites as they looked in the past.
  • Full Form: Internet Archive (Wayback Machine).
  • Why it’s needed: If a criminal deletes their website or a tweet after a crime, the Wayback Machine might have a "Snapshot" of it.
  • Archive.is: A similar tool that is great for saving "Social Media" pages.
  • Legal Value: Courts in India often accept archived links if you can prove they haven't been tampered with.
  • Investigation Use: Proving a suspect did have a certain post on their profile yesterday.
  • Frequency: Popular sites are archived every day; small scam sites might only be archived once.
  • Manual Save: You can go to web.archive.org and paste a link to "Save this page now" for future evidence.
  • General Location: archive.org or archive.is.
  • How to read it: It shows a calendar. Click a blue circle on a specific date to see the old version of the site.

10. Sock Puppets (Undercover Profiles)

  • What it is: A "Fake" but realistic social media account used by police to monitor suspects without them knowing.
  • Why it’s needed: Suspects will block a profile that looks like a cop. They will accept a friend request from a "Regular Person."
  • The "Persona": You must create a believable background (Interests, Friends, old posts).
  • Safety: Never use your real phone number or real photo for a sock puppet.
  • VPN: Always use a VPN when using a sock puppet so the suspect can't "IP Log" the police station.
  • Burner Phone: Use a dedicated phone for these accounts.
  • Investigation Use: Joining private Facebook groups or Telegram channels where illegal activity happens.
  • Engagement: Do not "bait" or "trap" suspects into committing a crime they weren't going to do (Entrapment).
  • Verification: Some platforms require a photo ID. This is where advanced OSINT skills are needed.
  • Key Rule: A sock puppet is a tool for observation, not for personal use.

11. Video Metadata (GPS Extraction)

  • What it is: Hidden data (EXIF) inside a video or photo file that records the exact GPS coordinates of where it was shot.
  • Why it’s needed: To find a suspect's "Hideout" from a video they uploaded to TikTok or YouTube.
  • The "Scrub": Most apps like WhatsApp and FB "scrub" (delete) this data when you upload.
  • The Exception: If you find the original file on a suspect's phone or a cloud drive (Google Photos), the GPS is usually there.
  • Tools: "ExifTool" or online "Metadata Viewers."
  • Data Included: Camera model, Date/Time, and Latitude/Longitude.
  • Investigation Use: Finding where a hostage video was filmed or where a drug stash is located.
  • General Location: Right-click a file -> Properties -> Details.
  • Google Maps: You can copy the Latitude/Longitude and paste them into Google Maps to see the house.
  • How to read it: GPS: 28.6129, 77.2295.

12. Dark Web Navigation (Tor/Onion)

  • What it is: A part of the internet that requires special software (Tor Browser) to access. Sites end in .onion.
  • Why it’s needed: This is the "Black Market" of the internet. Drugs, weapons, and stolen data are sold here.
  • Anonymity: Everything is encrypted and IPs are hidden through three layers of "Relays."
  • Investigation Use: Monitoring "Leaked Databases" (like the recent BigBasket or Air India leaks) to see if Indian citizens' data is being sold.
  • Marketplaces: Sites like "AlphaBay" (now gone) or "Dread" (a forum) are where criminals talk.
  • Safety: Never use your personal PC for this; use a "Virtual Machine" or a dedicated "Clean" laptop.
  • No Google: You cannot "Google" the dark web. You must use directories like "The Hidden Wiki."
  • Crypto Connection: Almost all dark web transactions happen in Bitcoin or Monero.
  • Tor Browser: Looks like Firefox but has a "purple" logo and extra security.
  • Key Rule: Accessing the dark web is not illegal, but buying things there is.

13. Discord Forensics

  • What it is: A chat app used by gamers, but also by hackers and extremist groups for secret communication.
  • Why it’s needed: Young criminals use Discord because it allows large "Servers" with private voice and text channels.
  • Servers vs. DMs: A "Server" is a group; "DMs" are private messages.
  • User ID: Every user has a unique 18-digit ID (e.g., 123456789012345678). This never changes even if they change their name.
  • Investigation Use: Finding "Raiding" groups that organize cyberattacks.
  • Bot Logs: Discord servers often use "Bots" to manage users. These bots keep logs of who joined and from which IP.
  • LEO Request: Discord has a specific portal for police to get subscriber info.
  • General Location: discord.com.
  • Screen Sharing: Suspects often "stream" their screen to others while hacking.
  • How to read it: It's organized into "Channels" like #general, #hacks, #chat.

14. Botnet Tracking ("Like Farms")

  • What it is: A network of thousands of "zombie" social media accounts controlled by one person.
  • Why it’s needed: To identify "Political Misinformation" or "Fake Trends" on Twitter/X.
  • The Pattern: If 5,000 accounts post the exact same spelling mistake at the exact same second, it’s a botnet.
  • Like Farms: Physical rooms in countries like China, India, or Vietnam where thousands of phones are rigged to "Like" posts.
  • Investigation Use: Finding the "Operator" who is being paid to destroy someone's reputation online.
  • Common Clues: Accounts with no profile pic, random strings of numbers in the handle (e.g., @Rahul12837465), and 0 followers.
  • Botometer: An OSINT tool that gives a "Score" on how likely an account is to be a bot.
  • API Abuse: Bots use the "API" to post automatically.
  • Suspension:Reporting these accounts to the platform can stop a fake trend from going viral.
  • Evidence: You need to show the "Network Map"—how all these fake accounts are connected.

15. Email Spoofing & SMTP Logs

  • What it is: Making an email look like it came from "bank@sbi.com" when it actually came from a scammer.
  • Full Form: SMTP (Simple Mail Transfer Protocol).
  • Why it’s needed: To find the Real Sender of a fake or threatening email.
  • Email Header: The hidden part of an email. In Gmail, click "Three Dots" -> "Show Original."
  • Received-From: This line in the header shows the Real IP Address of the computer that sent the mail.
  • X-Mailer: Shows what software was used (e.g., "PHP Mailer" or "Outlook").
  • SPF/DKIM: Security checks. If an email fails these, it’s likely "Spoofed" (Fake).
  • Return-Path: Where the email goes if it bounces. Scammers often forget to hide their real email here.
  • Investigation Use:Tracing a "Bomb Threat" email back to the original server.
  • How to read it: It looks like a wall of text. Press Ctrl+F and search for Received.
